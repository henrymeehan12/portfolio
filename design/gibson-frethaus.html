Gibson Frethaus
The Relic Messenger

A custom electric guitar built from scratch for Gibson U.S.A. Nashville. Hand-wound p90 pickups, nitrocellulose lacquer, a shape that borrows from the 1958 Korina Explorer. Aged on purpose.

Role
Designer / Builder
Client
Gibson Guitars U.S.A. Nashville
Year
Spring 2023

The studio started with a factory tour in Nashville and a visit to the Gibson Garage, where Mark Agnesi walked us through the story of the 1958 Korina Explorer — a guitar that flopped on release and got rediscovered twenty years later when hard rock caught up to it. That was the one that stuck with me.

I was digging through research when a Tom Petty track came on and sent me down a rabbit hole on Mike Campbell's Les Paul. Aged, overwound p90s, clear bobbins, hardware left exposed — I wanted that. I called Tom Murphy at Gibson's Murphy Lab to ask about aging technique, and the shape came together as a mix of the Korina Explorer's fast lines and a Les Paul's curves.

Two pickups, both custom. The neck is a Lollar p90 nickel staple. Lollar earned their reputation and the staple magnets aren't sold separately, so that was the call. The bridge I wound myself — clear bobbins I laser-cut from Plaskolite Optix, 42 AWG poly-coated copper from Stewmac, somewhere around 50,000 wraps per pickup. You have to keep the spool about three feet from the bobbin or the wrap doesn't lay right. I learned that the hard way.

Body is CNC'd quilted maple over African mahogany. I wrapped the binding using a method I watched at the factory, stained it in layers, burst the finish, then sprayed nitrocellulose clear coat. The lacquer cracks as it ages, which was the point.

I call it the Relic Messenger. It's the guitar Mike Campbell doesn't know he wants.

Materials — African mahogany back · Quilted maple top · CNC body
Electronics — Hand-wound 1968-style p90 bridge · Lollar p90 neck
Finish — Nitrocellulose clear coat
Hardware — Bigsby vibrato tailpiece
